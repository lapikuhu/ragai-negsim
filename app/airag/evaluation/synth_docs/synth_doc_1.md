# Five Essential Principles of Negotiation

Negotiation is often presented as a contest in which one person wins and the other gives something up. In practice, it is usually closer to solving a shared puzzle under pressure. Each side has different goals, limits, fears, and pieces of information. A good negotiator tries to understand how those pieces fit together before deciding what to ask for, what to offer, and when to walk away.

The following five principles provide a useful foundation for everyday negotiations.

## 1. Know What You Really Need

Before entering a negotiation, it helps to separate what you would like from what you actually need.

People often begin with a preferred outcome and treat it as if it were the only acceptable one. This can make them rigid. A student selling a used laptop may want €600, but that does not necessarily mean €600 is the lowest price they would accept. The preferred result, the realistic result, and the minimum acceptable result may all be different.

Think of this like planning a trip. You may prefer a direct flight at noon, but you might still accept an evening flight with one stop. What matters is knowing which inconveniences are tolerable and which ones make the trip no longer worthwhile.

Negotiators who understand their priorities can make concessions without becoming confused or resentful. They can give up something less important while protecting something that matters more.

### Example

Maria is negotiating the schedule for a group project. She would prefer to meet on Wednesday afternoon, but her real concern is avoiding meetings after 8 p.m. Instead of insisting on Wednesday, she explains that she is flexible about the day but cannot attend late at night.

The group chooses Thursday at 6 p.m. Maria does not receive her first choice, but her main need is protected.

## 2. Understand the Other Side’s Interests

A person’s stated demand is not always the same as the reason behind it.

Someone may ask for a lower price because they have a limited budget, because they believe the product is overpriced, or because they want to feel that they negotiated successfully. These situations may sound similar, but they call for different responses.

Imagine seeing smoke coming from a building. The smoke is visible, but it is not the source of the problem. In negotiation, demands are often the smoke. Interests, concerns, pressures, and motivations are closer to the fire.

Asking questions can reveal what is driving the other side. Once those reasons are clearer, the negotiators may discover several possible solutions instead of arguing over one demand.

### Example

A student asks a landlord to reduce the monthly rent by €100. The landlord refuses because the rent covers the mortgage and building costs.

After further discussion, the landlord learns that the student’s main concern is the high cost of moving in. Instead of reducing the monthly rent, the landlord allows the security deposit to be paid in three installments.

The rent stays the same, but the student’s immediate financial pressure is reduced.

## 3. Create Options Before Dividing Value

Many negotiations become unnecessarily difficult because both sides focus on a single number.

When people argue only about price, every gain for one side appears to be a loss for the other. However, agreements often contain several elements: timing, quality, responsibility, convenience, risk, payment method, future work, and access to resources.

A negotiation can be compared to packing a suitcase. If one item does not fit, the answer is not always to remove it. You might fold it differently, move another item, or use a different compartment. In the same way, negotiators can rearrange the terms of an agreement.

This does not mean that every negotiation can produce a perfect win for everyone. Sometimes the available value really is limited. Still, exploring multiple options before making final concessions usually leads to better results.

### Example

A freelance student designer asks for €500 to create posters for a university event. The student association can pay only €350.

Instead of ending the discussion, they consider other terms. They agree on €350, a public credit for the designer, permission to include the work in a portfolio, and free access to the event.

The designer receives less money than requested but gains exposure and useful portfolio material. The association stays within budget.

## 4. Use Information Carefully

Negotiation depends heavily on information. The more accurately you understand the situation, the less likely you are to accept a poor deal or make an unrealistic demand.

Useful information may include market prices, deadlines, competing offers, available alternatives, costs, rules, and the priorities of the other side. Preparation reduces the need to guess.

At the same time, negotiators should be careful about revealing information too quickly. Sharing your interests can help create solutions, but announcing your absolute limit at the beginning may weaken your position.

This is similar to playing a card game. You need to understand the rules and watch what others are doing, but you do not normally place your entire hand face up on the table.

Good information use does not require deception. It means deciding what to ask, what to verify, what to share, and when to share it.

### Example

Leon is buying a second-hand bicycle. Before meeting the seller, he checks the prices of similar bicycles and learns that most sell for between €220 and €280.

The seller asks for €350 and says the bicycle is in excellent condition. Leon notices that the tires will soon need replacing. He explains what he found and offers €250.

Because his offer is supported by evidence rather than guesswork, the discussion becomes more realistic. They eventually agree on €270.

## 5. Maintain a Credible Alternative

A negotiation becomes dangerous when one side believes that reaching any agreement is better than leaving without one.

Having another option reduces pressure. The alternative does not need to be ideal; it only needs to be good enough that you are not forced to accept unacceptable terms.

Think of negotiation as crossing a bridge. If it is the only bridge available, you may feel compelled to cross even if it looks unsafe. If there is another route, even a longer one, you can judge the bridge more calmly.

A credible alternative also helps a negotiator decide when to stop. Walking away should not be used as a dramatic threat unless the person is genuinely prepared to do it.

### Example

Nikos receives an internship offer from a company, but the position is unpaid and requires a long daily commute. He asks whether the company can provide a transport allowance.

Before the discussion, he applies to two other internships and receives an interview invitation from one of them. This second opportunity is not guaranteed, but it gives him another possible path.

When the company refuses any transport support, Nikos decides not to accept immediately. He continues with the other application instead of agreeing out of fear that no alternative exists.

## Bringing the Principles Together

These five principles are closely connected.

Knowing your real needs helps you judge possible offers. Understanding the other side’s interests helps you develop useful options. Good information helps you evaluate those options realistically. A credible alternative protects you from accepting an agreement that does not meet your minimum needs.

Negotiation is therefore not mainly about speaking aggressively, making clever threats, or forcing quick concessions. It is a process of preparation, questioning, comparison, and decision-making.

A strong negotiator does not simply ask, “How can I get what I want?”

They also ask:

“What matters most here?”

“What does the other person need?”

“What other arrangements are possible?”

“What facts am I missing?”

“What will I do if we do not agree?”

Those questions do not guarantee success, but they make careless decisions much less likely.
