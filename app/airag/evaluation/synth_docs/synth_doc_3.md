# Søren Kierkegaard: A Summary of His Main Propositions

Søren Kierkegaard was a nineteenth-century Danish philosopher, theologian, and religious writer whose work focused less on constructing a comprehensive philosophical system than on examining how an individual should actually live. His central concern was human existence as it is experienced from the first-person perspective: through choice, uncertainty, responsibility, anxiety, commitment, failure, and faith.

Kierkegaard believed that philosophy becomes inadequate when it treats human beings primarily as abstract objects of knowledge. A philosophical system may organize concepts and describe reality in a logically coherent way, but it cannot make decisions on behalf of a living person. The individual must still choose how to act, what to value, whom to trust, and what kind of life to pursue.

This emphasis on concrete existence contributed greatly to the later development of existential philosophy. Kierkegaard’s writings repeatedly return to the idea that human life is unfinished, unstable, and resistant to complete theoretical explanation. An existing person is never merely a concept, because existence always involves movement, possibility, conflict, and responsibility.

One of Kierkegaard’s major philosophical targets was the systematic idealism associated with G. W. F. Hegel. He believed that large philosophical systems risked presenting reality as if it could be viewed from a complete and detached perspective. In Kierkegaard’s view, however, the philosopher who constructs the system remains a finite individual who must continue living outside the conceptual structure they have created.

Kierkegaard therefore distinguished between abstract knowledge and existential understanding. A person may understand an ethical principle intellectually while failing to embody it in conduct. Similarly, someone may know Christian doctrines, attend church, and speak confidently about theology without allowing those beliefs to transform the way they live.

This distinction lies behind Kierkegaard’s famous emphasis on subjectivity. His statement that “truth is subjectivity” is often misunderstood as a claim that every personal opinion is equally true. That is not his position. Kierkegaard did not deny objective facts or suggest that sincerity can make a false proposition correct.

Rather, he argued that certain truths, especially ethical and religious truths, matter through the way an individual relates to them. In these areas, truth is not merely something to be observed from a distance; it must be appropriated inwardly and expressed through commitment, passion, sincerity, and action.

A person may hold a doctrinally correct belief in a detached, complacent, or dishonest manner. Another person may possess a less complete intellectual understanding but relate to the issue with genuine seriousness and commitment. For Kierkegaard, the quality of this relationship to truth is often more significant than the mere possession of correct information.

This concern is especially important in religious life. Faith cannot be reduced to memorizing propositions or accepting a set of historical claims. It involves a transformation in the individual’s orientation toward existence, responsibility, guilt, hope, and God.

Kierkegaard explored several broad ways in which people may organize their lives. These are commonly described as the aesthetic, ethical, and religious spheres, stages, or modes of existence. They should not be understood as fixed developmental steps through which everyone automatically progresses, but as distinct orientations toward life.

The aesthetic mode is centered on pleasure, novelty, possibility, and immediate experience. The aesthete seeks stimulation and attempts to avoid boredom, repetition, painful responsibility, and binding commitment.

This way of living need not be crude or openly hedonistic. An aesthetically oriented person may be highly educated, culturally refined, artistically sensitive, and intellectually sophisticated. What defines the aesthetic life is not vulgar pleasure, but the tendency to treat experience as something to be enjoyed without accepting lasting responsibility.

The aesthete prefers to keep possibilities open because commitment appears to close off alternatives. Relationships, careers, beliefs, and obligations may therefore be approached experimentally rather than decisively.

At first, this freedom can appear attractive. Over time, however, endless possibility may become empty because nothing acquires continuity or lasting significance. Without commitment, experiences remain disconnected, and the individual may become trapped in boredom, repetition, irony, or despair.

Kierkegaard’s figure of the seducer illustrates this problem. The seducer values anticipation, manipulation, and pursuit more than genuine relationship. Once possibility becomes reality, the excitement fades, and the individual must seek another experience to replace it.

The ethical mode of existence is organized around responsibility, continuity, duty, and self-commitment. The ethical person does not merely move from one interesting experience to another, but chooses to become a definite and accountable self.

For Kierkegaard, ethical life involves accepting obligations that remain binding even when moods and desires change. The individual recognizes that a meaningful life requires coherence across time and that present choices affect both future possibilities and past responsibilities.

Marriage is one of Kierkegaard’s recurring examples of ethical commitment. Romantic attraction may begin as a feeling, but marriage transforms that feeling into a continuing task that requires loyalty, repetition, patience, and responsibility.

The ethical individual does not assume that a commitment becomes invalid whenever enthusiasm weakens. Instead, the commitment is renewed through action and maintained through conscious choice.

Choice is therefore central to Kierkegaard’s philosophy. To choose is not merely to select between external options, as one might choose between products or destinations. Through significant decisions, a person helps determine what kind of self they will become.

Refusing to choose does not preserve complete freedom. Indecision is itself a form of choice because it allows habits, circumstances, social expectations, and other people to shape one’s life in the absence of deliberate commitment.

Kierkegaard frequently speaks of the need to “choose oneself.” This does not mean inventing an identity without limits or treating the self as a purely self-created project. Rather, it means accepting responsibility for one’s concrete history, circumstances, relationships, abilities, failures, and obligations.

A person does not begin from nothing. Ethical selfhood involves acknowledging the life one has actually been given and becoming responsible for how that life is interpreted and developed.

Yet the ethical sphere is not Kierkegaard’s final position. Ethical life reveals human dignity and responsibility, but it also exposes human limitation. Even a sincere person who attempts to live responsibly encounters guilt, inconsistency, weakness, and failure.

The ethical individual eventually discovers that self-mastery is incomplete and that moral effort alone cannot fully reconcile the self. This recognition opens the possibility of the religious mode of existence.

Religious existence concerns the individual’s relationship with God. Kierkegaard believed that this relationship cannot be fully mediated by social institutions, public opinion, philosophical systems, or collective identity.

At the deepest level, the individual stands alone before God. Kierkegaard often refers to this person as the “single individual.”

The idea of the single individual does not imply selfish isolation or indifference toward others. It means that no crowd, institution, or social group can assume responsibility for a person’s deepest commitments.

Public agreement cannot replace personal accountability. A person cannot become ethical or religious merely by belonging to a respectable community or repeating what others believe.

Kierkegaard was therefore deeply suspicious of “the crowd.” Crowds can weaken individual responsibility because people may feel less accountable when their actions are shared by many others.

Individuals sometimes participate collectively in conduct they would hesitate to defend personally. Public opinion can also encourage conformity by rewarding socially acceptable appearances rather than inward seriousness.

The crowd may turn difficult ethical and religious questions into fashionable opinions, political slogans, or conventional habits. Kierkegaard believed that truth is often encountered by the individual precisely when social approval is absent.

His criticism of the crowd was closely connected to his attack on established Christianity in Denmark. Kierkegaard argued that Danish society had confused being born into a Christian culture with becoming Christian in an existential sense.

Church membership, respectable conduct, familiarity with doctrine, and participation in religious customs had become substitutes for personal commitment. Kierkegaard called this cultural arrangement “Christendom.”

He sharply distinguished Christendom from genuine Christianity. In his view, Christianity is demanding, disruptive, and transformative rather than socially convenient.

It requires the individual to imitate Christ, not merely admire him as a historical or religious figure. This demand cannot be satisfied through conventional respectability alone.

Kierkegaard believed that religious institutions sometimes protected people from the seriousness of Christianity by softening its demands. When faith becomes socially comfortable, it loses its capacity to challenge the individual.

His writings therefore present faith as difficult rather than reassuring. Faith is not simply intellectual agreement with sufficient evidence; it involves commitment under conditions of uncertainty.

Kierkegaard explores this most famously through the biblical story of Abraham and Isaac in *Fear and Trembling*. Abraham is commanded by God to sacrifice his son Isaac, even though Isaac is also the child through whom God’s promise is supposed to be fulfilled.

From the perspective of ordinary ethics, Abraham’s intended action appears indefensible. Nevertheless, Abraham trusts that God’s promise will somehow remain true, even though he cannot rationally explain how.

Kierkegaard calls Abraham a “knight of faith.” This figure embodies a form of trust that persists despite contradiction, uncertainty, and the impossibility of public justification.

The point of the story is not that individuals are free to violate moral rules whenever they claim divine authority. Kierkegaard presents Abraham as an exceptional, terrifying, and paradoxical case rather than as a general model for ordinary conduct.

The story reveals a possible tension between universal ethical obligation and absolute religious obligation. Kierkegaard describes this tension as the “teleological suspension of the ethical.”

The phrase suggests that, in Abraham’s case, the ordinary ethical requirement is suspended in relation to a higher religious purpose. However, Kierkegaard does not provide a safe method for identifying or verifying such a suspension.

That absence is part of the story’s difficulty and danger. Abraham cannot explain himself in terms that others could reasonably accept, because his situation cannot be translated into universal ethical language.

He must therefore endure silence, anxiety, and isolation. His faith cannot be validated by public approval or philosophical proof.

Faith appears, in this sense, as a paradox. It cannot be made completely secure through objective certainty, because complete certainty would remove the need for faith.

Kierkegaard is often associated with the idea of a “leap of faith,” although the exact phrase is not always used in the way later summaries suggest. The leap should not be understood as thoughtless belief or a rejection of reason.

It refers instead to the transition from reflection to commitment. Reflection can clarify evidence, alternatives, risks, and possibilities, but it cannot eliminate every uncertainty from an existential decision.

At some point, the individual must either commit or remain indefinitely suspended. No amount of analysis can transform every important decision into a purely technical calculation.

This structure is not limited to religious faith. Marriage, trust, vocation, friendship, and ethical responsibility also require commitments that extend beyond complete evidence.

Human freedom therefore gives rise to anxiety. Kierkegaard distinguishes anxiety from ordinary fear because fear usually has a specific object, such as illness, punishment, failure, or loss.

Anxiety is more indefinite. It arises from the individual’s awareness of possibility and freedom.

Kierkegaard famously describes anxiety as the “dizziness of freedom.” A person recognizes that several courses of action are possible and that they may choose badly.

Anxiety reveals that human beings are not mechanically determined, yet it also shows that freedom provides no guarantee of safety or success. The individual must act without complete certainty.

For this reason, anxiety is not merely negative. It can educate the individual by revealing freedom, responsibility, and the possibility of becoming a self.

At the same time, anxiety can lead to avoidance, paralysis, distraction, or self-deception. A person may attempt to escape freedom by allowing others to decide, by hiding behind convention, or by refusing to commit.

Kierkegaard’s analysis of despair is equally important. In *The Sickness Unto Death*, despair is described not simply as sadness or emotional suffering, but as a disorder within the structure of the self.

The self, in Kierkegaard’s account, is not a fixed substance or object. It is a relation that relates itself to itself.

Human beings exist through tensions between finitude and infinitude, necessity and possibility, temporality and eternity. They are limited by concrete circumstances, yet capable of imagining alternatives and relating themselves to ideals.

A healthy self does not eliminate these tensions but holds them together appropriately. Despair occurs when the relation becomes distorted or unbalanced.

One form of despair consists in failing to recognize that one has a self. A person may live entirely through social roles, routines, expectations, and external achievements without developing inward awareness.

Such a person may appear successful, stable, and content while remaining spiritually disconnected from themselves.

Another form of despair involves refusing to be oneself. The individual becomes aware of some aspect of their identity but attempts to escape their history, limitations, guilt, dependency, or responsibility.

A further form involves defiantly willing to be oneself without dependence on God. In this condition, the person attempts to become completely self-created, self-grounding, and self-sufficient.

For Kierkegaard, these forms of despair share a common failure: the self does not relate properly to the source that established it.

The self becomes properly grounded only when it relates itself transparently to God. Despair is therefore not merely a psychological state, but a spiritual condition.

This explains why despair can exist without conscious unhappiness. A person may feel satisfied, admired, and successful while still being in despair because the problem lies in the structure of the self rather than in immediate emotion.

Kierkegaard’s account of sin develops from this understanding of despair. Sin is not merely the violation of an external rule, although it can include such violations.

More fundamentally, sin is a condition of misrelation before God. It involves the individual’s refusal, distortion, or failure to become properly grounded in the relationship that constitutes the self.

Awareness of standing before God intensifies personal responsibility because the individual can no longer hide behind convention, ignorance, or collective identity.

At the same time, Christianity introduces the possibility of forgiveness. The individual cannot overcome despair through willpower, self-discipline, or ethical achievement alone.

Reconciliation requires grace. Kierkegaard therefore rejects both moral complacency and moral self-sufficiency.

Human beings remain responsible for their choices and must strive to live ethically, yet they cannot save themselves entirely through effort.

Kierkegaard’s method of writing is closely connected to these philosophical concerns. He frequently published works under pseudonyms, each of which represented a particular viewpoint, personality, or mode of existence.

These pseudonymous authors should not automatically be treated as direct expressions of Kierkegaard’s own final position. They often disagree with one another and present partial or limited perspectives.

This method is known as indirect communication. Kierkegaard believed that existential understanding cannot simply be transferred from one person to another in the same way as factual information.

A teacher can explain a mathematical formula directly, but cannot simply command another person to become inwardly responsible, faithful, or ethically committed.

Instead, the writer may present possibilities, contradictions, characters, stories, and competing voices that force the reader to examine their own position.

Kierkegaard’s works therefore contain irony, parody, fictional editors, imagined diaries, speeches, dialogues, and unresolved tensions.

The reader is denied a single, comfortable system and must decide how to interpret the competing perspectives.

This literary method reflects Kierkegaard’s belief that existential truth requires personal appropriation. The reader must do more than understand the argument; they must determine what the argument demands of them.

Kierkegaard also distinguished recollection from repetition. Recollection looks backward and attempts to recover meaning from the past, whereas repetition moves forward by renewing meaning under changed circumstances.

True repetition is not mechanical duplication. It is the active reaffirmation of a commitment over time.

A marriage, friendship, vocation, ethical responsibility, or religious faith cannot survive through one initial decision alone. It must be repeatedly renewed as conditions change.

This idea challenges the aesthetic desire for constant novelty. Meaning does not always require a new experience; sometimes it requires a deeper and more responsible return to an existing commitment.

Kierkegaard’s account of love develops similar themes. In *Works of Love*, he distinguishes preferential forms of love, such as romantic love and friendship, from the Christian command to love one’s neighbor.

Romantic love and friendship naturally favor particular people. They depend partly on attraction, affinity, shared history, admiration, and emotional closeness.

Neighbor-love, by contrast, extends in principle to every person. The neighbor is not chosen because of attractiveness, usefulness, similarity, or personal preference.

To love one’s neighbor is to recognize a fundamental equality between human beings before God.

This does not mean that intimate relationships should disappear or that all people must be loved in exactly the same practical way. Rather, neighbor-love challenges selfishness, possessiveness, vanity, and exclusion within human relationships.

For Kierkegaard, Christian love is expressed through action rather than feeling alone. Feelings may change, weaken, or disappear, but the obligation to seek another person’s good remains.

This makes Kierkegaard’s account of love demanding. Genuine love cannot depend entirely on admiration, reciprocity, or emotional reward.

It also resists domination and unhealthy dependence. To love another person properly is not to possess them, but to help them become themselves before God.

Throughout his work, Kierkegaard contrasts outward appearance with inward reality. A socially respectable life may conceal despair, dishonesty, or avoidance.

Conversely, a person who appears unsuccessful or socially marginal may possess genuine inward integrity.

External achievements cannot fully reveal the quality of an individual’s existence. Wealth, status, knowledge, reputation, and public approval may coexist with profound self-deception.

This is why Kierkegaard gives such importance to intention and inwardness. However, intention alone is not sufficient.

Inward commitment must eventually appear in choices, habits, relationships, and conduct. Kierkegaard’s philosophy is therefore neither purely internal nor purely behavioral.

It concerns the integration of inward passion with outward action.

Kierkegaard also emphasizes the importance of the present moment. Human beings exist at the intersection of time and eternity, and certain moments can transform the meaning of an entire life.

Christianity, in his view, is not merely a historical doctrine concerning events in the distant past. The individual must become, in a spiritual sense, “contemporary” with Christ.

This means encountering the demand of Christianity as personally present rather than treating it only as an object of historical research.

Historical scholarship can provide valuable information about Christianity, but it cannot by itself produce faith.

Kierkegaard did not reject reason, evidence, or historical inquiry. He rejected the belief that these could eliminate the need for existential decision.

Reflection becomes dangerous when it serves as a permanent substitute for commitment. A person may continue gathering information because choosing feels risky.

Kierkegaard regarded modern intellectual culture as especially vulnerable to this problem. People may become skilled at understanding many perspectives while committing themselves to none.

Objectivity can become an excuse for detachment, irony can become an excuse for insincerity, and possibility can become an excuse for never acting.

Against this tendency, Kierkegaard insists that life must be lived forward even though it is often understood only backward.

Past events gain meaning through later interpretation, yet decisions must be made before their consequences are fully known.

Human existence therefore combines uncertainty with responsibility. A person must act without possessing a complete view of the future.

Kierkegaard does not promise a life without anxiety, suffering, contradiction, or failure. He treats these conditions as unavoidable features of becoming a self.

The task is not to eliminate every tension but to relate oneself truthfully and responsibly to those tensions.

His individualism should not be confused with the modern idea that authenticity means expressing every desire or doing whatever feels natural.

Kierkegaard does not assume that immediate desires reveal a person’s true identity. Those desires may themselves be forms of avoidance, self-deception, or aesthetic distraction.

Becoming oneself requires discipline, ethical responsibility, repetition, repentance, commitment, and, ultimately, a proper relationship with God.

Kierkegaard’s central question is therefore practical rather than merely theoretical: how should a particular human being exist?

His answer cannot be reduced to a simple formula, because existence requires individual decision and appropriation.

The individual must move from passive observation toward responsible participation.

They must recognize the limits of abstract knowledge and confront the anxiety that accompanies freedom.

They must distinguish temporary preferences from genuine commitments and resist losing themselves in public opinion.

They must acknowledge despair, guilt, limitation, and self-deception rather than hiding behind achievement or social respectability.

They must accept responsibility for their choices while recognizing the limits of ethical self-sufficiency.

Finally, they must become open to faith, forgiveness, grace, and transformation.

For Kierkegaard, the greatest danger is not necessarily making a visible mistake or suffering an obvious failure.

The deeper danger is living an entire life without becoming consciously responsible for one’s own existence.

A person may acquire knowledge, wealth, status, admiration, and influence while gradually losing the self.

Kierkegaard’s work therefore asks readers not merely to understand his concepts, but to examine the way they themselves are living.

That personal demand explains both the difficulty of his philosophy and its continuing influence.
