# Five Useful Negotiation Terms

## 1. BATNA

BATNA means **Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement**. It is the best realistic option available to you if the current negotiation ends without a deal.

A BATNA is not the outcome you hope to achieve in the negotiation. It is what you can actually do instead. For example, if you are negotiating a job offer, your BATNA might be accepting another offer, remaining in your current role, or continuing your job search.

### How it is used during a negotiation

Your BATNA helps you judge whether an offer is worth accepting. A proposal should usually be better than your best alternative; otherwise, there is little reason to agree.

A strong BATNA also reduces pressure. When you know that you have another workable option, you are less likely to accept poor terms out of fear. However, a BATNA is useful only if it is realistic. Imaginary alternatives do not improve your position.

## 2. Reservation Point

A reservation point is the least favorable deal you are willing to accept before choosing your alternative instead.

For a buyer, this might be the highest price they are prepared to pay. For a seller, it might be the lowest amount they are willing to receive. The reservation point can also include non-financial terms such as deadlines, responsibilities, guarantees, or working conditions.

### How it is used during a negotiation

The reservation point acts as a boundary. It helps you recognize when further concessions would make the agreement worse than walking away.

It should usually be decided before the negotiation begins, when you can think calmly. If you invent it during a stressful discussion, you may move the boundary simply because you feel pressure to reach an agreement.

A reservation point is normally kept private. Revealing it too early may allow the other side to offer only slightly better terms.

## 3. Target Point

A target point is the outcome you are realistically trying to achieve.

It is more ambitious than your reservation point but should still be supported by market information, preparation, or the circumstances of the negotiation. It represents a successful result rather than the absolute limit of what you would accept.

### How it is used during a negotiation

The target point gives direction to your offers and concessions. Without a target, negotiators often react to the other side instead of following a plan.

For example, a seller may hope to receive €1,000, aim realistically for €900, and refuse anything below €750. In that case, €900 is the target point and €750 is the reservation point.

The target should guide the negotiation, but it should not become an obsession. New information may justify changing it.

## 4. ZOPA

ZOPA means **Zone of Possible Agreement**. It is the range in which both sides could accept a deal.

Suppose a buyer is willing to pay up to €800 and a seller is willing to accept as little as €700. The possible agreement range lies between €700 and €800.

A ZOPA may involve more than price. It can include combinations of delivery dates, payment terms, responsibilities, service levels, or other conditions.

### How it is used during a negotiation

Negotiators try to discover whether a ZOPA exists without immediately revealing their exact limits.

Questions, offers, counteroffers, and market information help each side estimate the possible range. If there is no overlap, the parties may need to change the structure of the deal, add new terms, or conclude that no agreement is possible.

Understanding the ZOPA also prevents wasted effort. A negotiation cannot succeed through persuasion alone if the sides’ acceptable ranges do not overlap and no new value can be created.

## 5. Anchoring

Anchoring is the tendency for the first serious number or proposal in a negotiation to influence the rest of the discussion.

An opening offer can shape expectations, even when the number is somewhat arbitrary. Later proposals are often judged in relation to that first figure.

For example, if a seller begins at €1,500, an offer of €1,100 may appear low. If the opening price had been €1,200, the same €1,100 offer might appear reasonable.

### How it is used during a negotiation

A negotiator may use a well-supported opening offer to establish a favorable starting point. The anchor should be ambitious but credible. An extreme and unsupported number may damage trust or cause the other side to disengage.

When the other side anchors first, it is important not to accept their number as the natural center of the discussion. You can challenge the assumptions behind it, introduce independent evidence, or present a counter-anchor based on market data.

Anchoring is most effective when it is connected to a clear explanation rather than presented as a random demand.
